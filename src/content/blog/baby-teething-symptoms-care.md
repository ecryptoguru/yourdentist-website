Teething is a natural process, but it can make babies fussy and uncomfortable. Most babies start teething between 6 and 12 months, though some begin earlier or later. Understanding teething symptoms and knowing how to soothe your baby can help both of you through this phase.

## When do babies start teething?

Babies are born with 20 primary teeth below their gums. These teeth usually start to appear around 6 months of age. Most children have all 20 baby teeth by age 3.

## Baby teeth eruption timeline

| Age | Teeth that usually appear |
| --- | --- |
| 6 to 10 months | Lower central incisors |
| 8 to 12 months | Upper central incisors |
| 9 to 13 months | Upper lateral incisors |
| 10 to 16 months | Lower lateral incisors |
| 13 to 19 months | First molars |
| 16 to 23 months | Canines |
| 23 to 33 months | Second molars |

## Common teething symptoms

- Drooling more than usual
- Chewing on fingers, toys, or anything they can reach
- Swollen or tender gums
- Irritability and fussiness
- Slight rise in temperature (less than 38°C)
- Trouble sleeping
- Decreased appetite
- Rubbing ears or cheeks
- Redness in the cheeks

## Symptoms that are NOT caused by teething

High fever, diarrhoea, and severe rashes are not caused by teething. If your baby has these symptoms, consult a paediatrician.

## Safe teething remedies

### Teething rings

A cold (not frozen) teething ring can soothe sore gums. Make sure it is clean and made of safe material.

### Cold washcloth

Wet a clean washcloth, chill it in the refrigerator, and let your baby chew on it.

### Gum massage

Wash your hands and gently massage your baby's gums with a clean finger.

### Cold foods

For babies eating solids, cold foods like yoghurt or mashed chilled fruit can provide relief.

### Pain relief

If your baby is very uncomfortable, consult your paediatrician about using infant paracetamol. Avoid aspirin.

## What to avoid during teething

- Teething necklaces (choking risk)
- Frozen teething rings (can damage gums)
- Hard objects that could injure the mouth
- Honey before age 1
- Teething gels containing benzocaine (can be dangerous)
- Rubbing alcohol on gums

## Caring for baby teeth

### Clean before teeth appear

Wipe your baby's gums with a soft, damp cloth after feedings. This removes bacteria and gets them used to oral care.

### Brush when teeth appear

Use a soft, infant toothbrush with a tiny smear of fluoride toothpaste. Brush twice a day.

### Avoid bedtime bottles

Do not put your baby to bed with a bottle of milk, formula, or juice. This can cause baby bottle tooth decay.

### First dental visit

Schedule your baby's first dental visit by their first birthday or within 6 months of their first tooth appearing.

## Teething and sleep

Teething discomfort is often worse at night because there are fewer distractions. A soothing bedtime routine, clean teething ring, and paediatrician-approved pain relief can help your baby sleep better.

## Common teething myths

- Myth: Teething causes high fever. Fact: High fever is not caused by teething.
- Myth: Teething causes diarrhoea. Fact: Diarrhoea is not a teething symptom.
- Myth: All babies drool excessively during teething. Fact: Drooling varies by child.
- Myth: Teething gels are safe for all babies. Fact: Benzocaine gels are not recommended for children under 2.
- Myth: You should not clean baby teeth until all have come in. Fact: Start cleaning as soon as the first tooth appears.

## Frequently asked questions

**When do babies start teething?**
Most babies start teething around 6 months, but it can begin as early as 3 months or as late as 12 months.

**How long does teething last?**
Each tooth usually causes discomfort for a few days before and after it appears. The entire teething process can last until age 3.

**Can teething cause fever?**
Teething may cause a slight temperature rise, but high fever is not caused by teething. See a doctor for high fever.

**What can I give my baby for teething pain?**
Teething rings, cold washcloths, gum massage, and paediatrician-approved pain relievers are safe options.

**When should my baby first see a dentist?**
By their first birthday or within 6 months of the first tooth appearing.

**Is it normal for teething to cause a runny nose?**
A mild runny nose can accompany teething due to increased drooling, but a persistent runny nose with thick mucus is likely a cold and should be evaluated by a doctor.

**Can I use teething powder for my baby?**
Some traditional teething powders are not recommended as they may contain ingredients that are not safe for infants. Always consult your paediatrician before using any teething product.

**What if my baby refuses to eat during teething?**
Offer soft, cool foods and extra fluids. If your baby refuses to eat or drink for more than a day, consult your paediatrician.

**Are late teeth a concern?**
Some babies are late teethers and this is usually normal. However, if no teeth have appeared by 18 months, schedule a dental visit to rule out any underlying issues.

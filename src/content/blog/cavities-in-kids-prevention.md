Children are at higher risk because their enamel is thinner and they often consume more sugary foods and drinks. ## Why do children get cavities?

### Sugary diet

Frequent consumption of candies, chocolates, soft drinks, juices, and packaged snacks feeds bacteria that cause cavities.

### Poor brushing habits

Children may not brush properly or for long enough. They often miss back teeth and areas near the gumline.

### Thin enamel

Baby tooth enamel is thinner than permanent tooth enamel, making it easier for cavities to form.

### Bottle feeding at bedtime

Milk, formula, or juice in a bedtime bottle can cause baby bottle tooth decay. The sugar stays on teeth all night.

### Not visiting the dentist early

Many parents wait until a child has pain before visiting a dentist. By then, cavities may already be advanced.

## Signs of cavities in children

- White, brown, or black spots on teeth
- Tooth pain or sensitivity
- Pain while eating or drinking
- Bad breath that does not go away
- Swelling near the tooth
- Reluctance to eat
- Visible holes in teeth

If you notice any of these signs, visit YourDentist as soon as possible.

## Preventing cavities in children

### Start oral care early

Clean your baby's gums with a soft cloth even before teeth appear. Start brushing as soon as the first tooth erupts.

### Use the right amount of toothpaste

| Age | Toothpaste amount |
| --- | --- |
| Under 3 years | Rice-grain sized smear |
| 3 to 6 years | Pea-sized amount |
| Over 6 years | Pea-sized amount |

### Brush twice daily

Supervise brushing until your child can do it properly, usually around age 7.

### Limit sugary foods and drinks

Reduce candies, chocolates, soft drinks, and sweetened juices. Offer water between meals.

### Avoid bedtime bottles

Do not put your child to bed with a bottle containing anything other than water.

### Use dental sealants

Sealants protect the chewing surfaces of back teeth from decay.

### Regular dental visits

Take your child to the dentist every 6 months for checkups and cleaning.

## Treating cavities in children

### Fillings

Small cavities are removed and filled with tooth-coloured material.

### Pulpotomy

If decay reaches the pulp of a baby tooth, a pulpotomy may be needed. This is similar to a partial root canal for children.

### Crowns

For badly damaged baby teeth, stainless steel or zirconia crowns restore strength.

### Extraction

If a baby tooth cannot be saved, it may need to be removed to protect permanent teeth.

## Baby teeth cavities matter

Some parents think baby teeth do not need treatment because they fall out. However, baby teeth are important because they:

- Help children chew and speak
- Hold space for permanent teeth
- Guide permanent teeth into position
- Affect self-confidence
- Support normal facial development
- Prevent crowding of permanent teeth

Losing baby teeth early can cause alignment problems later, potentially requiring orthodontic treatment that could have been avoided.

## Foods that cause cavities vs foods that protect teeth

| Cavity-causing foods | Tooth-friendly alternatives |
| --- | --- |
| Candies and chocolates | Cheese and yoghurt |
| Soft drinks and colas | Water and milk |
| Sweetened juices | Fresh fruits |
| Packaged snacks and chips | Nuts and vegetables |
| Sticky sweets (chikki, jellies) | Sugar-free gum |

Encouraging tooth-friendly snacks and limiting sugary treats to mealtimes (rather than throughout the day) significantly reduces cavity risk.

## Cavity progression in children: stages and treatment

| Stage | What happens | Symptoms | Treatment needed |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Early demineralisation | White spots on enamel | None | Fluoride treatment, dietary changes |
| Enamel cavitation | Brown spot, small hole | Mild sensitivity | Filling |
| Dentin involvement | Cavity reaches dentin | Pain with sweet/cold | Filling or inlay |
| Pulp involvement | Decay reaches nerve | Spontaneous pain, swelling | Pulpotomy or pulpectomy |
| Abscess formation | Infection spreads beyond tooth | Severe pain, facial swelling | Extraction or root canal |

Early detection at regular checkups allows treatment at the earliest, least invasive stage. Dr. Dash uses child-friendly techniques to make each stage of treatment comfortable.

## Cavity prevention schedule by age

| Age | Prevention focus | What parents should do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 to 1 year | Gum care, first dental visit | Clean gums with cloth, schedule first checkup |
| 1 to 2 years | Brushing starts, diet control | Brush with rice-grain fluoride toothpaste |
| 2 to 4 years | Brushing supervision, fluoride | Supervise brushing twice daily, limit juice |
| 4 to 6 years | Sealants, regular checkups | Apply sealants on first molars at age 6 |
| 6 to 12 years | Independence with monitoring | Monitor brushing, apply sealants on second molars |
| 12+ years | Self-care, orthodontic check | Ensure consistent habits, regular dental visits |

Consistent prevention at each age stage dramatically reduces the risk of cavities and the need for extensive treatment.

## Frequently asked questions

**How common are cavities in children?**
Cavities are very common in children, but they are largely preventable with good habits and regular dental visits.

**Do baby teeth need fillings?**
Yes. Untreated cavities in baby teeth can cause pain, infection, and problems with permanent teeth.

**What causes cavities in toddlers?**
Frequent sugar exposure, bedtime bottles, poor brushing, and not visiting the dentist early are the main causes.

**How can I prevent cavities in my child?**
Limit sugar, brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste, avoid bedtime bottles, apply sealants, and visit the dentist regularly.

**Is fluoride safe for children?**
Yes, when used in the correct amount. Use a rice-grain sized smear for children under 3 and a pea-sized amount for children 3 to 6.

**Can cavities in baby teeth affect permanent teeth?**
Yes, untreated cavities in baby teeth can cause infections that spread to developing permanent teeth beneath them. Early loss of baby teeth can also cause crowding and alignment issues.

**How often should my child visit the dentist?**
Children should visit the dentist every 6 months for checkups and cleaning. Children with a history of cavities may need visits every 3 to 4 months.

**Are dental sealants safe for children?**
Yes, dental sealants are safe and highly effective. They protect the chewing surfaces of back teeth where most cavities form. Sealants are recommended for children as soon as permanent molars erupt.

**What should I do if my child has a cavity?**
Schedule a dental appointment as soon as possible. Early treatment prevents the cavity from growing and causing pain or infection.

**Can cavities spread between teeth?**
Yes, bacteria from one decayed tooth can spread to adjacent teeth, especially if oral hygiene is poor. Treating cavities promptly prevents spread to neighbouring teeth.

**Are silver fillings safe for children?**
At YourDentist, we use tooth-coloured composite fillings for children, which are safe, mercury-free, and aesthetically superior. Silver amalgam fillings are not recommended for children due to mercury concerns and visible appearance.

**How can I make my child enjoy brushing?**
Use flavoured toothpaste, colourful brushes, brushing apps with timers, and brush together as a family. Make it a fun routine rather than a chore. Dr. Dash can recommend child-friendly oral care products during your visit.

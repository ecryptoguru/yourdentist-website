## Why do children fear the dentist?

Children may fear the dentist because of:

- Unfamiliar environment and sounds
- Fear of pain
- Negative stories from others
- Previous bad dental experience
- Separation anxiety
- Shy or sensitive temperament
- Not knowing what to expect

Understanding the cause helps parents address it effectively.

## How to prepare your child for a dental visit

### Start early

Take your child to the dentist by their first birthday or within 6 months of their first tooth. Early visits help them get used to the dental environment.

### Use positive language

Avoid words like "pain," "shot," "drill," or "hurt." Use simple, positive words like "clean," "strong," and "healthy."

### Read books or watch videos

Children's books and videos about dental visits can help them understand what will happen.

### Role-play at home

Pretend to be the dentist and patient. Use a toothbrush to count teeth and show them that the visit is simple.

### Stay calm

Children pick up on parental anxiety. Stay relaxed and positive about the visit.

### Schedule wisely

Book appointments when your child is well-rested and fed. Avoid nap times or busy afternoons.

### Bring a comfort item

A favourite toy or blanket can help your child feel secure.

## What to do during the dental visit

### Be a calm presence

Stay with your child during the examination. Hold their hand if needed.

### Use short, honest explanations

Dr. Dash will explain procedures in child-friendly language. You can reinforce this at home.

### Praise cooperation

Praise your child for sitting still, opening their mouth, or being brave. Small rewards after the visit also help.

### Avoid bribery for bad behaviour

Do not promise sweets or toys if your child behaves badly. This can create more anxiety.

## Age-specific strategies for reducing dental anxiety

Different age groups need different approaches. Here is what works best at each stage:

| Age group | Strategy | What to avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 to 3 years | Sit in parent's lap, keep visits short | Long procedures, separation from parent |
| 4 to 6 years | Use tell-show-do method, give simple explanations | Complex words, surprises |
| 7 to 10 years | Explain why dental care matters, involve them | Talking down to them, dismissing fears |
| 11 to 14 years | Respect privacy, explain procedures in detail | Treating like young children, lecturing |

## Signs your child is anxious about the dentist

- Crying or tantrums before appointments
- Refusing to open mouth
- Clinging to parents
- Stomach aches before visits
- Difficulty sleeping the night before
- Refusing to eat before the appointment
- Excessive questions about what will happen
- Trying to negotiate out of going

If you notice these signs, talk to Dr. Dash before the appointment so she can plan a calmer approach. She may suggest a desensitisation visit first, where your child simply meets the team, sits in the chair, and gets familiar with the environment without any treatment.

## Parent checklist: before, during, and after dental visits

| Phase | What parents should do | What to avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Before visit | Read books, role-play, stay positive | Sharing scary stories, using fear words |
| Day of visit | Bring comfort item, arrive early | Rushing, skipping meals |
| During visit | Stay calm, hold hand if needed | Hovering, showing anxiety |
| After visit | Praise bravery, small reward | Criticising if child was nervous |
| Between visits | Maintain brushing routine, talk positively | Using dentist as a punishment threat |
| Before next visit | Remind of positive past visit | Building up anxiety over days |

Following this checklist helps create a predictable, positive routine around dental visits that reduces anxiety over time.

## Frequently asked questions

**How can I calm my child before a dental visit?**
Use positive language, role-play at home, read books, and stay calm yourself.

**Should I stay with my child during the dental exam?**
Yes, most children feel more secure with a parent nearby.

**What should I not say to my child about the dentist?**
Avoid words like pain, needle, drill, or hurt. Keep the conversation positive.

**Can dental anxiety affect my child's oral health?**
Yes, anxious children may avoid dental visits, leading to untreated cavities and gum problems.

**Is laughing gas safe for children?**
Yes, nitrous oxide is safe and commonly used to help anxious children relax during dental treatment.

**At what age should I first take my child to the dentist?**
The first dental visit should happen by the child's first birthday or within 6 months of the first tooth appearing. Early visits are usually just checkups and help the child get comfortable with the dental environment.

**What if my child had a bad experience at another dentist?**
Dr. Dash is experienced in helping children who have had negative dental experiences. She uses a gradual approach, starting with simple visits and building trust over time.

**Can I be in the room during my child's treatment?**
Yes, parents are welcome to stay during appointments. Your presence provides comfort and reassurance.

**How long does a child's dental appointment take?**
A routine checkup takes 20 to 30 minutes. Treatment appointments may take longer, but Dr. Dash keeps them as short as possible for anxious children.

**What if my child refuses to open their mouth at the dentist?**
Dr. Dash uses gentle behaviour management techniques like tell-show-do and positive reinforcement. She never forces a child. If needed, she may schedule a desensitisation visit where no treatment is done, just building comfort. Over 1 to 2 visits, most children become cooperative.

**Can siblings attend dental appointments together?**
Yes, siblings can attend together. Seeing an older sibling have a positive visit often helps younger children feel more comfortable. Dr. Dash can see siblings in sequence during the same appointment slot.

**How often should my anxious child visit the dentist?**
Every 6 months for routine checkups. For very anxious children, Dr. Dash may recommend more frequent short visits to build comfort gradually. Consistency is key to overcoming dental anxiety.

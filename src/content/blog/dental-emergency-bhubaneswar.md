## What is a dental emergency?

A dental emergency is any situation that requires immediate dental treatment to relieve severe pain, stop bleeding, save a tooth, or treat infection.

## Common dental emergencies

### Severe toothache

Intense, persistent pain that does not go away with painkillers is an emergency. It may indicate an abscess, deep decay, or pulp infection.

### Knocked-out tooth

A tooth that has been completely knocked out due to trauma needs immediate treatment. The chances of saving the tooth are highest within 30 to 60 minutes.

### Broken or chipped tooth

A fractured tooth can expose the nerve and cause pain. Sharp edges can also cut the tongue and soft tissues.

### Dental abscess

An abscess is a painful, pus-filled infection. It can cause swelling, fever, and spread to other areas if not treated quickly.

### Bleeding gums or soft tissue injury

Injuries to the lips, cheeks, tongue, or gums that cause heavy or persistent bleeding need urgent care.

### Lost filling or crown

A lost filling or crown can expose sensitive tooth structure and cause pain.

### Broken braces or wires

Loose or broken wires can poke the cheeks and gums, causing pain and injury.

## What to do in a dental emergency

### What to do for a knocked-out tooth

1. Pick up the tooth by the crown, not the root.
2. Rinse gently with milk or saline if dirty. Do not scrub.
3. Try to place the tooth back in the socket.
4. If not possible, store it in milk, saliva, or a tooth preservation solution.
5. Get to a dentist within 30 to 60 minutes.

### What to do for severe toothache

- Rinse with warm salt water
- Floss gently to remove trapped food
- Take pain medication as directed
- Apply a cold compress to the cheek
- See a dentist as soon as possible

### What to do for a broken tooth

- Save any broken pieces
- Rinse your mouth with warm water
- Apply gauze if there is bleeding
- Use a cold compress to reduce swelling
- See a dentist immediately

### What to do for a dental abscess

- Do not try to drain it yourself
- Rinse with warm salt water
- Take pain medication if needed
- See a dentist immediately
- Go to the emergency room if swelling affects breathing or swallowing

## When to go to the emergency room

Go to the hospital emergency department if you have:

- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Severe facial swelling
- High fever with dental infection
- Jaw fracture
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Signs of spreading infection

## Preventing dental emergencies

- Wear a mouthguard during sports
- Avoid chewing hard objects like ice and bones
- Do not use teeth to open bottles or packages
- Maintain regular dental checkups
- Treat cavities and cracks early
- Wear a nightguard if you grind your teeth
- Avoid sticky foods that can pull out fillings or crowns
- Keep your dentist's emergency number saved in your phone

## How diabetes affects oral health

### Gum disease

High blood sugar weakens the immune system and makes it harder for the body to fight infections. This increases the risk of gum disease. Diabetes also slows healing, making gum treatment more challenging.

### Dry mouth

Diabetes can reduce saliva flow. Dry mouth increases the risk of cavities, gum disease, and infections.

### Thrush

Diabetic patients are more likely to develop oral thrush, a fungal infection that causes white patches and soreness in the mouth.

### Slow healing

After dental procedures, people with diabetes may heal more slowly. This makes proper aftercare especially important.

### Taste changes

Some diabetic patients notice changes in taste, which can affect appetite and nutrition.

### Cavities

High sugar levels in saliva feed bacteria and increase the risk of tooth decay.

## Gum disease and diabetes: a two-way relationship

Gum disease and diabetes affect each other. Gum disease makes blood sugar harder to control. High blood sugar worsens gum disease. Treating gum disease can improve blood sugar control in people with diabetes.

## Signs of gum disease in diabetic patients

- Red, swollen, or bleeding gums
- Persistent bad breath
- Receding gums
- Loose teeth
- Pus between gums and teeth
- Pain while chewing

## Dental care tips for people with diabetes

### Control blood sugar

Keeping blood sugar within target range reduces the risk of gum disease and infections.

### Brush twice daily

Use a soft-bristle brush and fluoride toothpaste. Brush gently along the gumline.

### Floss daily

Flossing removes plaque between teeth and reduces gum inflammation.

### Use antibacterial mouthwash

A dentist-recommended mouthwash can reduce bacteria in the mouth.

### Stay hydrated

Drink water throughout the day to combat dry mouth.

### Avoid smoking

Smoking increases the risk of gum disease and diabetes complications.

### Visit the dentist every 3 to 6 months

Diabetic patients may need more frequent dental checkups and cleanings.

## Dental procedures and diabetes

Most dental procedures are safe for diabetic patients. However, timing and precautions may be needed:

- Schedule procedures when blood sugar is stable
- Take medications as usual unless advised otherwise
- Eat normally before appointments
- Bring glucose tablets if at risk of low blood sugar
- Follow aftercare instructions carefully
- Monitor blood sugar before and after dental work
- Avoid dental work during periods of poor sugar control

## How HbA1c levels affect gum disease risk

| HbA1c level | Blood sugar control | Gum disease risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Below 5.7% | Normal | Low |
| 5.7 to 6.4% | Pre-diabetes | Moderate |
| 6.5 to 7.5% | Well-controlled diabetes | Moderate to high |
| 7.6 to 9.0% | Fairly controlled | High |
| Above 9.0% | Poorly controlled | Very high |

Patients with poorly controlled diabetes are three times more likely to develop severe gum disease than those with good control. Improving blood sugar control can significantly reduce gum inflammation and slow disease progression.

## Diabetes medications and oral health

Some diabetes medications can affect oral health:

| Medication type | Oral health effect |
| --- | --- |
| Metformin | May cause dry mouth in some patients |
| Sulfonylureas | May alter taste sensation |
| Insulin | Rapid changes in blood sugar can affect healing |
| SGLT2 inhibitors | May increase risk of oral thrush |

Always inform Dr. Dash about all medications you are taking so she can adjust your dental treatment plan accordingly.

## Oral infections and diabetes

Diabetic patients should watch for signs of oral infection, including:

- Swelling
- Redness
- Pain
- Pus
- Fever
- Bad taste in the mouth

Oral infections can spread more quickly in diabetic patients and need prompt treatment.

## Frequently asked questions

**Does diabetes affect teeth?**
Yes, diabetes increases the risk of gum disease, cavities, dry mouth, and oral infections.

**Can gum disease affect diabetes?**
Yes, gum disease can make blood sugar harder to control. Treating gum disease may improve blood sugar levels.

**How often should diabetic patients visit the dentist?**
Diabetic patients should visit every 3 to 6 months for checkups and cleanings.

**Is dental treatment safe for diabetic patients?**
Yes, most dental treatments are safe. Inform your dentist about your condition and medications.

**How can I prevent gum disease if I have diabetes?**
Control blood sugar, brush twice daily, floss daily, avoid smoking, and visit the dentist regularly.

**Can laser gum treatment help diabetic patients?**
Yes, laser gum treatment is particularly beneficial for diabetic patients because it is less invasive, causes less bleeding, and promotes faster healing compared to traditional gum surgery.

**What HbA1c level is safe for dental treatment?**
An HbA1c below 7.5% is generally considered safe for most dental procedures. For levels above 9%, Dr. Dash may recommend postponing non-emergency treatment until blood sugar is better controlled.

**Does dry mouth from diabetes cause cavities?**
Yes, dry mouth reduces saliva, which normally protects teeth from decay. Diabetic patients with dry mouth should drink water frequently, use sugar-free gum, and ask Dr. Dash about saliva substitutes or fluoride treatments.

**Can improving gum health help control diabetes?**
Yes, research shows that treating gum disease can improve blood sugar control. Reducing gum inflammation helps the body respond better to insulin.

## What is a dental emergency?

A dental emergency is any problem that needs immediate attention to stop pain, prevent infection, or save a tooth. Common emergencies include:

- Severe toothache that does not respond to pain medication
- Knocked-out or broken tooth
- Swelling in the gums, face, or jaw
- Bleeding that does not stop
- Lost filling or crown
- Object stuck between teeth
- Dental abscess
- Injury to the mouth, lips, or tongue

If you are unsure whether your problem is an emergency, call us. We can guide you over the phone.

These symptoms need quick treatment. Delaying care can lead to more serious infection, tooth loss, or hospitalisation.

## What to do during common dental emergencies

### Severe toothache

Rinse your mouth with warm salt water. Gently floss around the tooth to remove any trapped food. Take an over-the-counter pain reliever if you are not allergic. Do not place aspirin directly on the gum. Call the clinic to book an urgent appointment.

### Knocked-out tooth

Handle the tooth by the crown, not the root. Rinse it gently with clean water if dirty, but do not scrub. Try to place it back in the socket and hold it with a clean cloth. If that is not possible, keep it in milk or saliva. Get to a dentist within 30 to 60 minutes for the best chance of saving it.

### Broken or chipped tooth

Rinse your mouth and save any broken pieces. Apply a cold compress to reduce swelling. If the tooth is sharp, cover it with dental wax or sugar-free gum to protect your tongue and cheek. See a dentist as soon as possible.

### Swelling or abscess

Do not apply heat to the outside of your face. Rinse with warm salt water and take a pain reliever. Swelling often means infection, which needs professional treatment. Antibiotics alone may not solve the problem.

### Lost filling or crown

Keep the crown safe. You can temporarily reattach it with over-the-counter dental cement if you cannot see a dentist immediately. Avoid chewing on that side. See a dentist within a day or two.

### Object stuck between teeth

Try to remove it with dental floss. Do not use sharp objects like pins or knives, which can damage gums. If floss does not work, call the clinic.

## When to go to a hospital instead of a dentist

Some emergencies need hospital care, not a dental clinic. Go to the nearest hospital emergency department if you have:

- Difficulty breathing or swallowing
- Swelling that closes your eye or throat
- Severe facial trauma
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Jaw fracture
- High fever with severe infection

These are medical emergencies that require hospital resources.

## Preventing dental emergencies

Not all emergencies can be prevented, but regular care reduces the risk:

- Visit the dentist every six months for checkups.
- Treat cavities before they reach the pulp.
- Wear a mouthguard during sports.
- Avoid chewing hard objects like ice and popcorn kernels.
- Do not use your teeth to open bottles or packages.
- Maintain good oral hygiene to prevent infections.

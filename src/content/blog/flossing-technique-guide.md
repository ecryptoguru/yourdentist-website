## Why flossing is important

Plaque builds up between teeth every day. If not removed, it hardens into tartar and causes:

- Cavities between teeth
- Gingivitis (early gum disease)
- Periodontitis (advanced gum disease)
- Bad breath
- Receding gums
- Tooth loss

Flossing once a day prevents these problems.

## How to floss properly: step by step

### Step 1: Take enough floss

Use about 18 inches (45 cm) of dental floss. Wind most of it around your middle fingers, leaving 1 to 2 inches for flossing.

### Step 2: Hold the floss correctly

Hold the floss tightly between your thumbs and index fingers. Keep a small section taut.

### Step 3: Guide the floss gently

Slide the floss between your teeth using a gentle back-and-forth motion. Do not snap the floss into your gums.

### Step 4: Curve around the tooth

Curve the floss into a C-shape around one tooth. Glide it under the gumline and up the side of the tooth.

### Step 5: Move to a clean section

Use a fresh section of floss for each tooth. This prevents spreading bacteria.

### Step 6: Floss behind the back teeth

Do not forget the back teeth. The gum pockets behind the last molars often collect plaque.

## Common flossing mistakes

- Flossing too aggressively and cutting gums
- Skipping the back teeth
- Reusing the same section of floss
- Flossing too quickly
- Stopping when gums bleed
- Using too little floss
- Snapping the floss into the gumline

## Should you floss before or after brushing?

Flossing before brushing is often recommended because it loosens plaque and debris, allowing fluoride from toothpaste to reach between teeth. However, flossing at any time is better than not flossing at all.

## Flossing with braces

Flossing with braces is more challenging but essential. Use:

- Floss threaders to pass floss under wires
- Orthodontic floss with stiff ends
- Water flossers to clean around brackets
- Interdental brushes for braces

## Flossing with dental implants

Floss around implants carefully. Use implant-safe floss or a water flosser. Avoid snapping the floss around implant crowns, as this can damage the gum tissue.

## How often should you floss?

Floss at least once per day. Flossing twice a day is even better for gum health. Consistency matters more than perfect technique.

## What if your gums bleed when flossing?

Some bleeding is normal when you start flossing. It usually stops within 1 to 2 weeks as your gums become healthier. If bleeding continues after 2 weeks, see your dentist. Persistent bleeding can be a sign of gum disease.

## Floss alternatives

If you find traditional floss difficult, try:

- Floss picks
- Water flossers
- Interdental brushes
- Air flossers
- Electric flossers

These are not always as effective as string floss but are better than not cleaning between teeth at all.

## Flossing for children

Children should start flossing as soon as two teeth touch each other, usually around age 2 to 3. Parents should floss for young children until they can do it themselves, usually around age 8 to 10. Use child-friendly floss picks to make it easier and more enjoyable.

## How to make flossing a habit

- Keep floss visible next to your toothbrush
- Floss at the same time every day, such as before bed
- Use floss picks if string floss feels awkward
- Set a reminder on your phone until it becomes routine
- Try flavoured floss if you find the taste unpleasant
- Reward yourself for flossing consistently for a month

## Daily flossing routine guide

| Time | Step | Duration | Key point |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Before brushing | Take 18 inches of floss | 10 seconds | Wind around middle fingers |
| Upper right | Floss upper right teeth | 30 seconds | Curve floss around each tooth |
| Upper left | Floss upper left teeth | 30 seconds | Clean below gumline gently |
| Lower right | Floss lower right teeth | 30 seconds | Use fresh section per tooth |
| Lower left | Floss lower left teeth | 30 seconds | Don't forget behind last molar |
| Front teeth | Floss front teeth carefully | 30 seconds | These are most visible |

Total time: about 2 to 3 minutes. Following this systematic approach ensures no teeth are missed and makes flossing a quick, efficient habit.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is flossing really necessary?**
Yes. Brushing cannot clean between teeth, where many cavities and gum problems start.

**How often should I floss?**
At least once a day, preferably before bedtime.

**Why do my gums bleed when I floss?**
Bleeding usually means your gums are inflamed. It should improve within 1 to 2 weeks of regular flossing. If it continues, see a dentist.

**Can I floss with braces?**
Yes, but you need floss threaders or a water flosser to clean under the wires.

**What is better: water flosser or string floss?**
String floss is more effective for removing plaque. Water flossers are helpful for braces, implants, and hard-to-reach areas.

**Can flossing whiten teeth?**
No, flossing does not whiten teeth. However, it removes plaque and food particles that can make teeth look dull and cause stains between teeth.

**Is it normal for teeth to feel different after flossing?**
Yes, teeth may feel cleaner or slightly spaced after flossing because plaque between them has been removed. This is normal and healthy.

**Can I reuse floss picks?**
Floss picks are designed for single use. Reusing them spreads bacteria and reduces effectiveness. Use a fresh pick each time.

**How long should I spend flossing?**
Aim for 2 to 3 minutes to clean between all teeth thoroughly. Rushing can leave plaque behind.

**Can flossing damage gums or cause recession?**
When done correctly, flossing does not damage gums. Gum recession from flossing only occurs with aggressive or improper technique. Always slide floss gently between teeth and curve it around the tooth rather than snapping it down into the gum.

**What type of floss is best for tight teeth?**
Waxed floss or dental tape slides more easily between tight teeth. If your teeth are very tight, try ultra-thin floss or floss threaders. Water flossers are also a good alternative for tight spaces.

**Should I floss if I have sensitive gums?**
Yes, but be extra gentle. Sensitive gums often benefit from soft floss or a water flosser. If sensitivity persists, see Dr. Dash to rule out gum disease or other issues. Flossing actually helps reduce gum sensitivity over time by removing irritants.

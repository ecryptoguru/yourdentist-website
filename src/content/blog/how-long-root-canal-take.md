## Step-by-step root canal timeline

### Step 1: Consultation and diagnosis (15 to 30 minutes)

Your first visit includes a discussion of your symptoms, a clinical examination, and digital X-rays. Dr. Arpita Dash checks the tooth, takes X-rays to see the root structure, and confirms whether root canal treatment is needed. This visit may also include a brief treatment plan and cost estimate.

### Step 2: Preparing for the procedure (10 to 15 minutes)

On the day of the root canal, we prepare the area. This includes:

- Applying local anesthesia
- Placing a rubber dam around the tooth
- Taking a final X-ray if needed
- Explaining the steps one more time

The anesthesia takes 5 to 10 minutes to fully numb the tooth.

### Step 3: Accessing the tooth (5 to 10 minutes)

A small opening is made in the top of the tooth to reach the pulp chamber. This is done with a dental drill. You should not feel pain, only vibration and pressure.

### Step 4: Removing the pulp and cleaning canals (20 to 45 minutes)

The infected pulp is removed with rotary instruments. The canals are then cleaned, shaped, and disinfected. The time depends on the number of canals:

- Front tooth: 1 canal, usually 20 to 30 minutes
- Premolar: 1 to 2 canals, usually 30 to 40 minutes
- Molar: 3 to 4 canals, usually 40 to 60 minutes

### Step 5: Disinfecting with laser (5 to 10 minutes)

At YourDentist, we use laser-assisted disinfection to kill bacteria in the canal system. This step improves the success rate and reduces the chance of re-infection.

### Step 6: Filling and sealing the canals (10 to 15 minutes)

The clean canals are filled with gutta-percha and sealed with a biocompatible cement. This prevents bacteria from re-entering the tooth.

### Step 7: Temporary or permanent filling (10 to 15 minutes)

The access opening is closed with a temporary or permanent filling. If a crown is needed, we may place a temporary filling and take impressions for the crown.

## Single-sitting vs multiple-sitting root canal

Most root canals at YourDentist are completed in a single sitting. Multiple visits may be needed in these cases:

- Severe infection with swelling or abscess
- Pus drainage that needs medication first
- Complex canal anatomy requiring extra time
- Patient preference or medical reasons

If multiple visits are needed, the first visit focuses on cleaning and medication, and the second visit completes the filling.

## Time between root canal and crown

After root canal treatment, the tooth needs a crown to protect it. The timeline is usually:

| Step | Time |
| --- | --- |
| Root canal treatment | Same day |
| Impression for crown | 1 to 7 days after root canal |
| Crown fabrication | 3 to 7 days |
| Crown placement | 1 to 2 weeks after root canal |

Some crowns can be made same-day with CAD-CAM technology.

## Factors that make root canal take longer

- **More canals:** Molars take longer than front teeth.
- **Curved canals:** Curved or blocked canals need careful handling.
- **Severe infection:** Infection may require medication between visits.
- **Previous root canal failure:** Retreatment is more complex.
- **Calcified canals:** Narrow or hardened canals need special instruments.

## Can I return to work after a root canal?

Yes, most patients return to work the same day or the next day. If you had sedation or feel tired, you may prefer to rest. Avoid heavy physical activity for the first 24 hours.

## Eating after a root canal

Wait until the anesthesia wears off before eating. Start with soft foods for the first day. Avoid chewing on the treated side until the permanent crown is placed. Hard, sticky, or very hot foods should be avoided initially.

## Why single-sitting root canals are preferred

Single-sitting root canals are convenient because they:

- Save time for the patient
- Reduce the number of injections
- Lower the risk of contamination between visits
- Get the patient out of pain faster
- Reduce travel for patients coming from Cuttack, KIIT, or Patia

## Time investment vs value

A root canal may take 60 to 90 minutes, but it saves a tooth that could otherwise be lost. Without treatment, the infection can spread, leading to abscess, bone loss, and possible tooth extraction. The time spent on a root canal is a small investment compared to the long-term benefit.

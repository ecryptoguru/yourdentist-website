Mouthwash is a popular oral hygiene product. It can freshen breath, reduce bacteria, and provide fluoride. However, many people use mouthwash incorrectly, thinking it replaces brushing and flossing. Mouthwash is a helpful addition, not a substitute. Studies show that using mouthwash alongside brushing and flossing can reduce plaque by up to 20% more than brushing alone. The Indian Dental Association recommends mouthwash as a supplementary oral hygiene measure, not a primary one.

## What mouthwash can do

- Freshen breath
- Reduce plaque bacteria
- Strengthen enamel with fluoride
- Reduce gingivitis
- Help heal mouth sores
- Reduce sensitivity

## What mouthwash cannot do

- Remove plaque between teeth
- Remove tartar
- Treat cavities
- Replace brushing and flossing
- Whiten teeth dramatically

## How to use mouthwash correctly

### Step 1: Brush and floss first

Use mouthwash after brushing and flossing. This removes plaque and food first, allowing the mouthwash to reach clean surfaces.

### Step 2: Measure the right amount

Use the amount recommended on the label, usually 15 to 20 ml.

### Step 3: Swish for the right time

Swish for 30 to 60 seconds. Do not swallow.

### Step 4: Spit out

Spit the mouthwash out completely. Do not rinse with water immediately after, as this can wash away the active ingredients.

### Step 5: Wait before eating

Wait 30 minutes before eating or drinking after using fluoride or antibacterial mouthwash.

## Should you use mouthwash before or after brushing?

Most dentists recommend using mouthwash after brushing and flossing. This allows fluoride or antibacterial agents to coat clean teeth.

## Mouthwash with alcohol vs alcohol-free

| Factor | Alcohol-based | Alcohol-free |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Kills bacteria | Strong | Effective |
| Burning sensation | Yes | No |
| Dry mouth | Can worsen | Better choice |
| Children | Not recommended | Safer |
| Long-term use | May irritate tissues | Gentler |

Alcohol-free mouthwash is better for people with dry mouth, sensitive gums, or children.

## Mouthwash for children

Children under 6 should not use mouthwash unless recommended by a dentist because they may swallow it. For older children, use alcohol-free, child-friendly mouthwash and supervise them.

## Mouthwash for gum disease

Antibacterial mouthwashes containing chlorhexidine or cetylpyridinium chloride can help reduce bacteria and inflammation. Chlorhexidine is usually prescribed for short-term use because it can stain teeth with long-term use.

## Mouthwash for bad breath

Cosmetic mouthwashes mask bad breath temporarily. Antibacterial mouthwashes address the bacteria that cause bad breath. If bad breath persists, see a dentist to find the cause.

## Mouthwash for sensitive teeth

Desensitising mouthwashes contain ingredients like potassium nitrate or fluoride. They can help reduce mild sensitivity but are not a substitute for dental treatment.

## Common mistakes with mouthwash

- Using mouthwash instead of brushing
- Swallowing mouthwash
- Using too much
- Rinsing with water immediately after
- Using mouthwash right after using fluoride toothpaste (can wash away fluoride)
- Using alcohol-based mouthwash with dry mouth
- Using mouthwash immediately after eating (wait 30 minutes)
- Using cosmetic mouthwash for medical problems
- Not checking for alcohol content in products for children

## Frequently asked questions

**Can mouthwash replace brushing?**
No. Mouthwash cannot remove plaque and food particles like brushing and flossing.

**Which mouthwash is best?**
It depends on your needs. Fluoride for cavities, antibacterial for gum disease, desensitising for sensitivity, and alcohol-free for dry mouth.

**Should I rinse after mouthwash?**
No. Avoid rinsing with water for 30 minutes after using fluoride or antibacterial mouthwash.

**Can children use mouthwash?**
Children under 6 should not use mouthwash unless directed by a dentist. Older children should use alcohol-free mouthwash under supervision.

**How often should I use mouthwash?**
Once or twice daily, depending on the product and your dentist's recommendation.

**Can I use mouthwash every day long-term?**
Alcohol-free fluoride or cosmetic mouthwashes are safe for daily long-term use. Chlorhexidine mouthwash should only be used for short periods as prescribed by a dentist.

**Does mouthwash expire?**
Yes, mouthwash has an expiry date. Using expired mouthwash can reduce effectiveness and may cause irritation. Check the bottle before use.

**Can pregnant women use mouthwash?**
Alcohol-free mouthwashes are generally safe during pregnancy. However, always consult your doctor and dentist before using any new product during pregnancy.

**Can mouthwash cure gum disease?**
No, mouthwash cannot cure gum disease on its own. It can help reduce bacteria as part of a treatment plan that includes professional cleaning and possibly laser therapy.

**Can I use mouthwash after tooth extraction?**
Yes, but not immediately. Wait 24 hours after extraction, then use a gentle saltwater rinse or prescribed chlorhexidine mouthwash. Avoid vigorous swishing for the first few days to protect the blood clot.

**Does mouthwash kill good bacteria too?**
Antibacterial mouthwashes can reduce both harmful and beneficial oral bacteria. This is why long-term use of chlorhexidine is not recommended. Alcohol-free fluoride mouthwashes are gentler on the oral microbiome and safer for daily use.

**Can I use mouthwash with braces?**
Yes, mouthwash is especially helpful for braces patients because it reaches areas that are difficult to clean. Use fluoride mouthwash daily to prevent white spot lesions around brackets. Dr. Dash can recommend the best mouthwash for braces patients.

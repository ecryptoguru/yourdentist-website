## How pregnancy affects oral health

### Hormonal changes

Increased levels of progesterone and estrogen can cause gums to swell, bleed, and become more sensitive to plaque. This condition is called pregnancy gingivitis.

### Morning sickness

Stomach acid from vomiting can erode enamel. Frequent nausea can also reduce brushing frequency.

### Cravings and diet

Cravings for sugary or acidic foods increase the risk of cavities.

### Reduced saliva

Some pregnant women experience dry mouth, which allows bacteria to grow faster.

## Pregnancy gingivitis

Pregnancy gingivitis causes red, swollen, and bleeding gums, especially during the second to eighth month. It affects up to 70% of pregnant women.

### Symptoms

- Gums bleed when brushing or flossing
- Swollen or tender gums
- Bad breath
- Gum recession in severe cases

### Treatment

- Professional cleaning
- Good brushing and flossing
- Antibacterial mouthwash if recommended
- More frequent dental visits

## Is dental treatment safe during pregnancy?

Yes, most dental treatments are safe during pregnancy. The second trimester is usually the safest time for routine dental work.

| Treatment | Safety |
| --- | --- |
| Dental cleaning | Safe in all trimesters |
| Fillings | Safe, preferably in second trimester |
| Root canal | Safe if needed, with precautions |
| X-rays | Only if necessary with shielding |
| Whitening | Best avoided during pregnancy |
| Elective procedures | Best postponed until after delivery |

## Oral care tips during pregnancy

### Brush twice daily

Use a soft-bristle brush and fluoride toothpaste. Brush gently to avoid irritating sensitive gums.

### Floss daily

Flossing removes plaque between teeth and helps prevent pregnancy gingivitis.

### Rinse after morning sickness

If you vomit, rinse your mouth with water or a mixture of baking soda and water. Wait 30 minutes before brushing to avoid damaging softened enamel.

### Eat a balanced diet

Limit sugary and acidic foods. Eat foods rich in calcium, vitamin D, and vitamin C for healthy teeth and gums.

### Stay hydrated

Drink plenty of water to prevent dry mouth and wash away bacteria.

### Visit the dentist

Schedule a dental checkup during your first trimester. Your dentist can monitor your oral health throughout pregnancy.

## Dental X-rays during pregnancy

Modern dental X-rays use very low radiation. If necessary, your dentist will use a lead apron and thyroid collar to protect you and your baby. Non-urgent X-rays can usually be postponed.

## Local anaesthesia during pregnancy

Local anaesthetics used in dentistry are generally safe during pregnancy. Dr. Dash uses pregnancy-safe medications and minimises any risk.

## Pregnancy tumours

Pregnancy tumours are small, red, swollen growths on the gums. They are not cancerous and usually go away after delivery. If they interfere with eating or cleaning, they can be removed safely.

## Trimester-by-trimester dental care guide

| Trimester | Dental care recommendations |
| --- | --- |
| First (1 to 3 months) | Schedule checkup, manage morning sickness, gentle brushing |
| Second (4 to 6 months) | Best time for cleanings, fillings, and safe treatments |
| Third (7 to 9 months) | Short appointments only, avoid lying flat in chair |
| Post-delivery | Resume normal dental care, address any pregnancy-related issues |

The second trimester is considered the safest and most comfortable time for dental treatment.

## How poor oral health affects your baby

Research has shown connections between maternal oral health and baby's health:

- Gum disease is linked to premature birth and low birth weight
- Cavity-causing bacteria can be transmitted from mother to baby
- Poor maternal nutrition affects baby's tooth development
- Pregnancy gingivitis can worsen if untreated, leading to periodontitis

Taking care of your oral health during pregnancy protects both you and your baby.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can I go to the dentist while pregnant?**
Yes, dental care is safe and important during pregnancy. The second trimester is the best time for routine treatment.

**Will pregnancy affect my teeth?**
Hormonal changes can make gums more sensitive and increase the risk of gingivitis. Good oral care helps prevent problems.

**Can I get a root canal while pregnant?**
Yes, if necessary. Dr. Dash uses pregnancy-safe medications and techniques.

**How can I prevent pregnancy gingivitis?**
Brush twice daily, floss daily, eat a balanced diet, and get regular dental cleanings.

**Is dental anaesthesia safe during pregnancy?**
Yes, local anaesthesia used in dentistry is considered safe during pregnancy.

**Can morning sickness damage my teeth?**
Yes, stomach acid from vomiting can erode enamel. Rinse with water or baking soda solution after vomiting, and wait 30 minutes before brushing.

**When should I visit the dentist during pregnancy?**
Schedule your first visit during your first trimester. The second trimester is ideal for any necessary treatment. Continue regular checkups throughout pregnancy.

**Can I whiten my teeth during pregnancy?**
Teeth whitening is best avoided during pregnancy as a precaution. Wait until after delivery and breastfeeding for cosmetic procedures.

**Will pregnancy cause me to lose teeth?**
No, the old myth that pregnancy causes tooth loss is false. With good oral hygiene and regular dental visits, your teeth will remain healthy throughout pregnancy.

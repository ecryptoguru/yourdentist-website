## Why brushing technique matters

Plaque is a sticky film of bacteria that forms on teeth every day. If not removed, it hardens into tartar and produces acids that cause cavities. It also irritates gums and leads to gingivitis and periodontal disease.

Brushing removes plaque from tooth surfaces. Flossing removes plaque from between teeth where a brush cannot reach. Mouthwash helps but does not replace brushing and flossing.

## How often should you brush?

Brush twice a day for two minutes each time. The morning brush removes overnight bacteria buildup. The night brush removes food particles and plaque before sleep, when saliva flow is lower and bacteria are more active.

Brushing more than three times a day or brushing too hard can wear down enamel and cause gum recession. Quality matters more than quantity.

## The correct brushing technique

Follow these steps for effective plaque removal:

1. **Use a soft-bristle brush.** Hard bristles can damage enamel and gums. Replace your brush every three months or when the bristles fray.
2. **Apply a pea-sized amount of fluoride toothpaste.** You do not need a lot. Children under six should use a rice-grain amount.
3. **Hold the brush at a 45-degree angle to the gums.** This helps the bristles reach under the gumline where plaque collects.
4. **Use gentle circular motions.** Do not scrub back and forth aggressively. Use small, light circles covering two to three teeth at a time.
5. **Clean all surfaces.** Brush the outer surfaces, inner surfaces, and chewing surfaces of every tooth.
6. **Use the toe of the brush for the front teeth.** Tilt the brush vertically and use up-and-down strokes for the inner front teeth.
7. **Brush your tongue.** Bacteria on the tongue cause bad breath. Use a gentle scraping motion.
8. **Rinse thoroughly.** Spit out the toothpaste and rinse with water. Do not swallow toothpaste.

## How long should you brush?

Two minutes is the recommended time. Many people think they brush for two minutes but actually brush for less than 45 seconds. Use a timer or an electric toothbrush with a built-in timer.

## Manual toothbrush vs electric toothbrush

Both can clean well if used correctly. Electric toothbrushes are helpful for people who brush too hard, have limited hand mobility, or need help timing their brushing. Many models have pressure sensors that warn you when you are pressing too hard.

Manual toothbrushes work fine with proper technique. The key is soft bristles, gentle pressure, and thorough coverage.

## Common brushing mistakes to avoid

| Mistake | Why it is harmful | What to do instead |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Brushing too hard | Wears enamel and causes gum recession | Use gentle pressure |
| Brushing too fast | Leaves plaque behind | Brush for two minutes |
| Skipping inner surfaces | Misses plaque on back of teeth | Clean every surface |
| Using hard bristles | Irritates gums and enamel | Use soft bristles |
| Brushing immediately after acidic food | Softens enamel, causes wear | Wait 30 minutes |
| Storing a wet brush covered | Grows bacteria | Let it dry uncovered |

## Should you brush before or after breakfast?

Brush before breakfast to remove overnight bacteria and reduce acid damage from food. If you prefer to brush after eating, wait at least 30 minutes, especially after acidic foods like oranges, tomatoes, or coffee. Acid softens enamel, and brushing too soon can wear it away.

## Toothpaste selection: what actually matters

Choose a toothpaste with fluoride. Fluoride strengthens enamel and helps prevent cavities. Beyond that, pick a toothpaste that addresses your needs:

- **Sensitivity:** Potassium nitrate or stannous fluoride
- **Tartar control:** Pyrophosphates
- **Whitening:** Mild abrasives or peroxide
- **Gum health:** Stannous fluoride or zinc

Avoid abrasive charcoal toothpastes for daily use. They can wear enamel over time.

## How to floss properly

Flossing removes plaque between teeth and below the gumline where brushing cannot reach.

1. Take about 18 inches of floss and wind most around your middle fingers.
2. Hold one to two inches between your thumbs and index fingers.
3. Gently slide the floss between teeth. Do not snap it into the gums.
4. Curve the floss into a C-shape around each tooth.
5. Slide it under the gumline and move it up and down.
6. Use a fresh section of floss for each tooth.

If traditional floss is difficult to use, try floss picks, interdental brushes, or a water flosser.

## How often should you visit the dentist?

Even with excellent home care, visit your dentist every six months for a checkup and professional cleaning. A dentist can remove tartar, spot early decay, and check for gum disease before it becomes serious.

## Best foods for healthy teeth

- **Crunchy vegetables** like carrots and celery clean teeth naturally.
- **Cheese and milk** provide calcium and neutralise acids.
- **Nuts** provide minerals for enamel.
- **Water** rinses away food and bacteria.
- **Leafy greens** are high in vitamins and low in sugar.

## Foods and drinks to limit

- Sugary snacks and sodas feed cavity-causing bacteria.
- Sticky candies cling to teeth for hours.
- Acidic drinks like citrus juices and soft drinks soften enamel.
- Frequent snacking reduces the time saliva has to repair enamel.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should I brush my teeth?**
Brush for two minutes, twice a day. Use a timer to make sure you are not rushing.

**Is an electric toothbrush better than a manual one?**
An electric toothbrush can help if you brush too hard or too fast, but a manual brush works well with proper technique.

**Should I floss before or after brushing?**
Either is fine. The important thing is that you floss daily.

**Can I brush too much?**
Yes. Brushing more than three times a day or brushing too hard can damage enamel and gums.

**What type of toothpaste should I use?**
Use a fluoride toothpaste. Choose one based on your needs, such as sensitivity, whitening, or gum health.

**Why do my gums bleed when I brush?**
Bleeding gums are usually a sign of gingivitis or brushing too hard. If it persists, see a dentist for a gum check.

**Is charcoal toothpaste safe for daily use?**
No, charcoal toothpaste is highly abrasive and can wear down enamel over time. Dr. Dash recommends fluoride toothpaste for daily use. Charcoal products should be avoided or used very occasionally.

**Should I brush before or after breakfast?**
Brushing before breakfast is ideal because it removes overnight bacteria and coats teeth with fluoride before acidic foods. If you prefer brushing after breakfast, wait at least 30 minutes to allow enamel to re-harden.

**How do I clean around braces or implants?**
For braces, use interdental brushes, orthodontic floss threaders, and a water flosser. For implants, use a soft brush, water flosser, and non-abrasive toothpaste. Dr. Dash provides specific cleaning instructions for patients with braces or implants.

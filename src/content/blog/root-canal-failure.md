## What is root canal failure?

Root canal failure occurs when the tooth does not heal properly after treatment, or when the infection returns. This can happen months or even years after the original procedure. It does not mean the original dentist made a mistake. Teeth are complex, and some infections are difficult to eliminate completely.

## Signs of root canal failure

Watch for these symptoms after a root canal:

- Pain that returns or worsens after treatment
- Swelling in the gums or face
- A pimple or bump on the gum near the treated tooth
- Tenderness when chewing or biting
- Discolouration of the tooth or gum
- Bad taste or smell in the mouth
- Fever with dental pain

If you notice any of these signs, see your dentist as soon as possible.

## Common causes of root canal failure

### Missed canals

Some teeth have extra, narrow, or curved canals that are hard to find during the first treatment. If bacteria remain in these canals, the infection can return.

### Incomplete cleaning

If the canals were not cleaned and shaped thoroughly, bacteria can survive and multiply.

### Recontamination after treatment

If the temporary filling falls out or breaks before the permanent crown is placed, bacteria can re-enter the tooth.

### Delayed crown placement

A root canal-treated tooth becomes weak. If a crown is not placed in time, the tooth can crack, allowing bacteria to enter again.

### New decay or crack

A new cavity or crack in the tooth can expose the root canal filling to bacteria, causing reinfection.

### Complex root anatomy

Some teeth have curved, branching, or calcified canals that are difficult to treat completely.

### Gum disease around the tooth

If gum disease affects the tissues around the tooth, infection can reach the root tip even after a successful root canal.

## Treatment options for failed root canals

### Root canal retreatment

Retreatment is the most common approach. The tooth is reopened, the old filling material is removed, the canals are cleaned again, and the tooth is resealed. Retreatment has a good success rate, especially when the original issue was missed canals or incomplete cleaning.

### Apicoectomy

If retreatment is not possible, an apicoectomy may be done. This minor surgery removes the tip of the root and seals the end of the root canal from the side. It is usually done when there is persistent infection at the root tip.

### Extraction

If the tooth cannot be saved, extraction may be the last option. The missing tooth can be replaced with a dental implant, bridge, or denture.

## Root canal retreatment procedure

### Step 1: Reopening the tooth

The tooth is numbed and reopened. The crown or filling is removed to access the root canals.

### Step 2: Removing old filling material

The old gutta-percha filling is removed using special instruments. This allows the dentist to clean the canals again.

### Step 3: Re-cleaning and disinfecting

The canals are cleaned, shaped, and disinfected. A microscope or magnifying tools may be used to find missed canals. Laser disinfection may also be used.

### Step 4: Refilling and sealing

The clean canals are filled and sealed again. A new temporary or permanent filling is placed.

### Step 5: Crown replacement

If the existing crown is damaged, it may need to be replaced to protect the tooth.

## Success rate of retreatment

Root canal retreatment has a success rate of 65% to 85%, depending on the cause of failure and the tooth condition. Apicoectomy has a similar success rate. Extraction and implant placement has a success rate of over 95% but involves more time and cost.

## How to prevent root canal failure

- Choose an experienced dentist for the first root canal.
- Make sure all canals are cleaned and sealed.
- Get a permanent crown as soon as recommended.
- Maintain good oral hygiene.
- Visit the dentist every six months.
- Avoid chewing hard objects on the treated tooth.
- Treat cavities and cracks immediately.

## Root canal failure risk factors

| Risk factor | Why it increases failure risk | How to mitigate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Delayed crown placement | Tooth cracks, bacteria re-enter | Get crown within 1 to 2 weeks |
| Complex tooth anatomy | Missed canals harbour bacteria | Choose experienced dentist with CBCT |
| Severe initial infection | More bacteria to eliminate | Complete full antibiotic course |
| Diabetes | Slower healing, higher infection risk | Control blood sugar before and after |
| Smoking | Impairs healing, increases infection | Stop smoking during treatment |
| Poor oral hygiene | New decay exposes root canal | Brush, floss, and attend checkups |
| Teeth grinding (bruxism) | Can crack treated tooth | Wear nightguard as recommended |

Understanding these risk factors helps patients take preventive steps to protect their root canal investment.

## Frequently asked questions

**What are the signs of root canal failure?**
Persistent pain, swelling, gum pimple, bad taste, and difficulty chewing can indicate root canal failure.

**Why do root canals fail?**
Failure can occur due to missed canals, incomplete cleaning, delayed crown placement, new decay, or complex tooth anatomy.

**Can a failed root canal be fixed?**
Yes, retreatment or apicoectomy can often save a tooth after a failed root canal.

**How long after a root canal can it fail?**
Failure can occur months or years later, often due to new decay, a cracked tooth, or missed canals.

**Is root canal retreatment painful?**
Retreatment is done under anesthesia and should not be painful. Mild soreness after is normal.

**When is extraction the only option?**
Extraction is recommended when the tooth is too damaged, has severe bone loss, or cannot be cleaned properly.

**Can root canal failure happen years after treatment?**
Yes, failure can occur years or even decades after a successful root canal. Common late causes include new decay, tooth cracks, gum disease, or gradual bacterial leakage. Regular dental checkups help detect these issues early.

**Is apicoectomy painful?**
Apicoectomy is done under local anaesthesia and is not painful during the procedure. Mild swelling and soreness for 2 to 3 days after surgery is normal. Most patients return to work the next day.

**Can I prevent root canal failure after treatment?**
Yes. Get the permanent crown placed promptly, maintain excellent oral hygiene, avoid biting hard foods on the treated tooth, attend 6-month checkups, and address any new dental issues immediately. These steps significantly reduce failure risk.

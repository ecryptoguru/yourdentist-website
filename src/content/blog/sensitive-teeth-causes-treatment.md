Tooth sensitivity is a common problem that affects millions of people worldwide. It causes a sharp, sudden pain when teeth are exposed to hot, cold, sweet, or acidic foods and drinks. Sensitivity usually happens when the protective enamel is worn down or gums recede, exposing the dentin underneath. Dentin contains microscopic tubules that connect to the tooth nerve, which is why temperature changes cause pain. Studies estimate that 1 in 8 adults experiences some form of tooth sensitivity.

## What causes tooth sensitivity?

### Enamel erosion

Acids from food, drinks, and stomach acid can wear away enamel. Citrus fruits, soft drinks, and frequent vomiting can contribute.

### Gum recession

When gums recede, the root surface becomes exposed. The root surface is softer than enamel and more sensitive.

### Tooth decay

Cavities expose the inner layers of the tooth, causing sensitivity.

### Cracked or chipped teeth

Cracks allow stimuli to reach the nerve, causing pain.

### Grinding or clenching

Bruxism wears down enamel and increases sensitivity.

### Aggressive brushing

Brushing too hard with a hard-bristle brush can wear enamel and cause gum recession.

### Whitening products

Some over-the-counter whitening products can cause temporary sensitivity.

### Recent dental treatment

Teeth may be sensitive after fillings, scaling, or whitening. This usually improves within a few days.

## How dentin hypersensitivity works

To understand sensitivity, it helps to know how teeth are structured:

1. **Enamel** is the hard outer layer that protects the tooth.
2. **Dentin** is the layer beneath enamel. It contains thousands of microscopic tubules.
3. **Pulp** is the innermost layer containing nerves and blood vessels.

When enamel is worn or gums recede, dentin is exposed. Temperature changes, sweet foods, and acidic drinks travel through the tubules to the nerve, causing sharp pain.

## Home remedies for sensitive teeth

- Use desensitising toothpaste twice daily
- Use a soft-bristle toothbrush
- Brush gently with circular motions
- Avoid acidic foods and drinks
- Rinse with water after consuming acidic items
- Wait 30 minutes after eating before brushing
- Use fluoride mouthwash
- Avoid whitening products if they cause sensitivity

## Preventing tooth sensitivity

- Brush twice daily with a soft brush
- Use fluoride toothpaste
- Floss daily
- Avoid acidic foods and drinks
- Do not brush immediately after consuming acid
- Wear a nightguard if you grind your teeth
- Visit the dentist every 6 months

## Sensitivity triggers and how to manage them

| Trigger | How it causes pain | Management tip |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Hot drinks | Heat reaches nerve through tubules | Let beverages cool slightly |
| Cold water | Cold reaches nerve through tubules | Use room temperature water |
| Sweet foods | Sugar triggers nerve response | Limit sweets, rinse after |
| Acidic foods | Erodes enamel further | Rinse with water after eating |
| Cold air | Reaches exposed root surfaces | Breathe through nose in winter |
| Brushing too hard | Wears enamel, causes recession | Use soft brush, gentle pressure |

Understanding your specific triggers helps you manage sensitivity and avoid the foods and habits that worsen it.

## Frequently asked questions

**Why are my teeth sensitive?**
Sensitivity is usually caused by enamel erosion, gum recession, cavities, or grinding.

**Can sensitive teeth be cured?**
Yes, depending on the cause. Desensitising toothpaste, fluoride, fillings, or bonding can treat most cases.

**Is tooth sensitivity a sign of a cavity?**
It can be. Localised sensitivity to sweets or cold may indicate a cavity.

**Does desensitising toothpaste really work?**
Yes, with regular use it can block the pathways that cause sensitivity.

**Should I see a dentist for sensitive teeth?**
Yes, especially if sensitivity is severe, lasts more than a week, or affects one tooth.

**Can teeth whitening cause sensitivity?**
Yes, whitening can cause temporary sensitivity for 1 to 3 days. Using desensitising toothpaste before and after whitening can help.

**Is tooth sensitivity permanent?**
No, most cases of sensitivity can be treated. However, if the cause is not addressed, it may return.

**Can gum recession cause sensitivity?**
Yes, receding gums expose the root surface, which is softer and more sensitive than enamel.

**What toothpaste is best for sensitive teeth?**
Desensitising toothpastes containing potassium nitrate or stannous fluoride are effective. Dr. Dash can recommend the best one for your case.

**Can sensitivity be a sign of something serious?**
Yes, sensitivity can indicate a cracked tooth, deep cavity, or dying nerve. If sensitivity is severe, localised to one tooth, or lingers after the trigger is removed, see Dr. Dash promptly for evaluation.

**How long does desensitising toothpaste take to work?**
Most desensitising toothpastes take 2 to 4 weeks of regular use to provide noticeable relief. For faster results, Dr. Dash can apply professional-grade desensitising agents in the clinic.

**Can I prevent sensitivity from whitening?**
Yes. Use desensitising toothpaste for 2 weeks before and after whitening. Dr. Dash also offers low-sensitivity whitening options at YourDentist for patients prone to sensitivity.
